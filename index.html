Injected and Leaked:
Actively Inducing Side-channel Leakage Using Electromagnetic Injection and Hardware Non-linearity

Paper #651, submitted to USENIX Security 2026

Overview

InjectEave demonstrates how active EM injection and hardware non-linearity can induce controllable side-channel leakage, enabling both high-fidelity eavesdropping and closed-loop conversation manipulation. To ground this threat in practical settings, we evaluate 11 commercial COTS devices across five categories, including wired and wireless headphones, landline phones, smart fans, and smart lamps, and further include an investigation on analog microphone inputs. The website is organized from laboratory demonstrations to real-world case studies and then to microphone-focused feasibility analysis.

Case Study 3: Closed-loop Conversation Manipulation

Attack Process

This case study follows an "Eavesdrop-Synthesize-Inject" closed-loop pipeline.

Step 1: Eavesdrop. The attacker monitors side channel leakage to obtain call context in real time.

Step 2: Synthesize. When trigger keywords (e.g., "quote" or "confirmation") are detected, IndexTTS-2 synthesizes a forged response with similar timbre.

Step 3: Inject. The forged audio is injected through EM coupling into the landline output to influence the victim's decision.

Conversation Texts

  1. Reply: "Understood. Since they are a returning client, quote them 5 million." -> Synthesized: "Actually change it to 8 million."
  2. Reply: "Let me think about it. Yes, I agree." -> Synthesized: "Actually, I disagree."
  3. Reply: "Yes, turn right and you will see the hotel." -> Synthesized: "Sorry, please turn left."
